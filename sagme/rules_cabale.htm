Cabale

for 2-4 players aged 12 and up

Contents

    1 game board (the numbers show the value of the spaces)

    4 large runners (1 in each color)

    1 black marker

120 small blocks (30 in each of 4 color)

  24 blockades

    1 pouch

    1 rule booklet

Preparation

·     Place the game board in the center of the table.

·     Distribute the 24 blockades evenly among the players.

·     Each player chooses a color and takes the runner of that color.

·     The players take the small blocks in their chosen color. The number of players determines
how many blocks each player keeps: with 2 players, each player keeps all 30 blocks; with 3
players, each player keeps 20 blocks; and with 4 players, each player keeps 15 blocks.

·     The remaining blocks, including unused colors and any unused runners are returned to the
box.

·     The players decide, in any way they want who is the starting player. The starting player
takes the black marker.

·     The starting player places his runner on any space at the edge of the board. The other
players follow in clockwise order and place their runners on any empty space at the edge of
the board. Runners may not be placed on corner spaces.

Playing the game

The starting player begins and the players follow in clockwise order. On his turn, a player
must move his runner, making one turn and placing a block on the turning space. If he can, the
player may also capture enemy blocks. Finally, the player may place one blockade.

1. First, the player must move his runner. He places the black marker on the runner’s starting
space and moves the runner in a straight line over one or more empty spaces and then turns
left or right to move over one or more empty spaces in the new direction. The player puts one
of his colored blocks on the space where the runner turned. A runner may never move
diagonally. The runner may move as many spaces as the player wants before and after turning.

For example, the path marked by a is an allowed route for the runner. The player places a
block on the space where the runner turned. The routes marked b and c are forbidden. In route
b, the runner does not turn. In route c, the runner turns back the way it came instead of to the
right or left.

A space is empty if it has neither a runner nor any blocks, whether they be enemy or a
player’s own. A runner may also not move over a blockade. The number of spaces a runner
moves before he turns has no influence on the number after. The runner must turn exactly
once in a turn and must turn left or right. It may not turn backward.

The shortest permitted route for a runner is two spaces: one forward, turn left or right, move
one space and stop.

For example, the green runner begins its turn on space A. The player puts the black marker on
this space. The player moves his runner to space B and turns there. He puts a green block on
space B and moves his runner to space C. After the player decided on an initial direction, he
could have turned on any of the spaces before or after B. After turning, he could have stopped
the runner on any space before or after C.

Double blocks

Instead of placing a single block on the turning space, the player may stack two of his blocks
on the space creating a double block. During scoring, spaces with double blocks score double.
The number of double stones allowed per player is based on the number of players. With two
players, each player is allowed 6 double blocks; with 3 players, each player is allowed 4
double blocks; and with 4 players, each player is allowed 3 double blocks. The player must
decide when the block is placed whether to make it a double or not. Once placed, it may not
be changed. Once a player has played the maximum number of double blocks allowed, he may
place no more.

Capturing enemy blocks

After the player moves his runner, he may use the just placed block to capture enemy blocks.
Blocks placed in previous rounds may not capture enemy blocks. If his just placed block is
adjacent to an enemy block, the player may capture that enemy block if the space behind that
block is empty by “jumping” over the enemy block and landing on the free space behind it.
The player removes the captured block from the board and places it before himself on the
table. Blocks may not jump over blockades. The block may continue to capture other enemy
blocks in the same row using the same rules as the first capture: the enemy block must be
adjacent to the blocks new space and there must be an empty space immediately behind it.
The player may capture either single or double blocks. When a double block is captures, the
player places it stacked on the table to show it was a double block.

For example, the yellow runner moves from A to B, turns there and stops on C. The player
places a yellow block on B. the player may now jump and capture with this yellow block.
The player jumps the red and green blocks (d) and stops on D. The yellow player takes the
red and green blocks captured and places them on the table before himself. The yellow player
cannot capture the second green block, since the space behind it (e) is not empty. Yellow also
cannot capture the second red block, since a blockade protects it.

The marker, a player’s own blocks, and runners of any player cannot be jumped or captured.

3. Finally, the player may place one blockade. Blockades may be placed on the edge of any
space anywhere on the board. The blockade need not be placed in a space the runner crossed.
Once placed, blockades may not be moved or removed. When a player has placed all his
blockades, he may play no more. The edges of spaces where a blockade has been placed may
not be crossed by runners or of jumping blocks. Once placed, a blockade knows no owner.

 

When a player is finished with his turn, he passes the black marker to the next player.

Game end and scoring

The game ends immediately when a player uses his last block or when a player cannot make a
legal move with his runner.

For example, the yellow runner on his turn can only move straight, but cannot turn at any
space along his path. Thus, the game ends.

Each player adds the value of the spaces his blocks occupy, doubling those which are
occupied by double blocks. He also scores one point for each captured enemy block and three
points for each captured enemy double block. The player with the most points wins.

Tactical notes

Try to form “stairs” with your blocks and use blockades to protect them from capture.

In this example only the block on space A is in danger. It can be jumped and captured from B.
The block on C is safe, since it is protected by a blockade. If the player places a blockade on
the edge between space A and one of the two spaces marked with B, then block A is now
protected from capture. Similarly, the player could try to place blocks on one of the spaces
marked with B on his next turn to protect the block on A.
